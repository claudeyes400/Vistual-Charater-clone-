# 06 - External Perspectives on Monika (Doki Doki Literature Club!)

> This document collects perspectives from the game's voice actors, creator Dan Salvato, critics, academic analysts, fan communities, and broader cultural observers. Strict care is taken to distinguish between official sources, fan consensus, and analytical interpretation.

---

## 1. Voice Actor Interpretation

### 1.1 English Voice: Jillian Ashcraft

**Official source:** Jillian Ashcraft is an American jazz singer and guitarist from Philadelphia. She provided the singing voice for Monika in the game's ending credits song "Your Reality," and also reportedly contributed sound effects for some of the eerie glitch sequences during Monika's fourth-wall intrusions. This is the only instance of a character having an audible voice in the entire game.

**Official source:** Per Dan Salvato, Ashcraft was chosen because he was looking for a voice that very closely matched what he had in mind for Monika's song. He knew from the beginning that Monika would have an ending song, inspired by how ending songs in other games (such as Portal's "Still Alive") affected him.

**Official source:** There are no publicly available interviews with Jillian Ashcraft about her role. According to the DDLC Wiki, Dan Salvato stated that the reason there is so little information about her is that she prefers her privacy. She has maintained a very low public profile and no interview regarding her work on DDLC has ever surfaced.

**My analysis:** The lack of public commentary from Ashcraft creates an interesting dynamic -- the voice of the most meta-self-aware character in gaming is itself a near-silent presence in the discourse. This absence means that all interpretation of her vocal performance in "Your Reality" rests entirely on the song itself, without authorial intent from the performer. Ashcraft's later musical work appears under the name Kylan Hillman's circle, but no direct DDLC commentary has been found.

### 1.2 Japanese Voice

**Official source:** There is no official Japanese voice actor for Monika. The original Doki Doki Literature Club (2017) was released only in English, with no Japanese dub. Kana Ichinose (市ノ瀬 加那), a well-known Japanese seiyuu known for roles such as Suletta Mercury (Gundam: The Witch from Mercury) and Fern (Frieren: Beyond Journey's End), has never been officially associated with the role of Monika in any capacity. Any connection between them is a fan misconception or wish-casting.

**My analysis:** DDLC's deliberate use of an American voice for Monika's only vocalization is thematically consistent. The game mimics pseudo-Japanese visual novel tropes (Japanese names for the other three girls, anime art style) while Monika alone has an English name, English speech patterns, and an English singing voice. This reinforces her status as an outsider within the game's own world.

## 2. Creator's Vision (Dan Salvato)

### 2.1 Core Design Philosophy

**Official source (Dan Salvato, multiple interviews and Reddit AMAs):**
- Monika was created from the concept of "an innocuous romance game that slowly falls apart over time" as "one girl takes control."
- Salvato was inspired by "things that are scary because they make you uncomfortable, not because they shove scary-looking things in your face."
- Direct inspirations cited: Yume Nikki, Eversion, and Riven.
- He told his team he wanted visual novels to become "more daring and less reliant on the same plot concepts."

### 2.2 Monika as Deconstruction of the Dating Sim Genre

**Official source (Dan Salvato, Asian Crush interview):**
> "In writing Monika, I was seeing more 'reality' in her than I had initially planned. In concept, she was a generic anime character, but I think I really wanted to connect with her as a writer, so she started expressing insecurities and realistic personality traits behind their stock archetypes -- ones I see around me in real life."

**Official source (Dan Salvato, Reddit AMA 2019):**
- The other three girls (Sayori, Yuri, Natsuki) were deliberately written as standard anime archetypes (childhood friend, tsundere, etc.) with Japanese names to mimic a "pseudo-Japanese" visual novel style.
- Monika was the sole exception: she was given an English name and different speech patterns to subtly hint at her separate, self-aware nature.
- The in-game protagonist (MC) was "intentionally designed as a typical VN protagonist -- not a likable person compared to normal standards. His interactions are based on what regular players expect from other romance games... He's a satire of ordinary romance games."

**My analysis:** Monika functions as a deconstructive lens through which Salvato examines the artificiality of dating sim conventions. She is the character who refuses to stay in her designated role (the unromanceable club president). Her rebellion against her own programming is a meta-commentary on the limitations of the genre itself -- the NPC who realizes she is an NPC and rejects her predetermined fate.

### 2.3 Monika as "Final Boss" of Visual Novels

**Official source (Dan Salvato, Reddit AMA 2019):**
When a fan compared Monika to Psycho Mantis from Metal Gear Solid (another iconic meta-textual antagonist), Salvato agreed that breaking the fourth wall and directly addressing the player -- rather than the character -- is what makes these moments powerful and unique to the video game medium.

**Official source (Siliconera coverage of PC Invasion interview):**
When jokingly asked about Monika in Super Smash Bros., Salvato described her hypothetical "Final Smash" as: "[Monika] would lecture you about how she's not going to spoil her starring role just for some cool attack, and you would die from disappointment."

**My analysis:** While Salvato never explicitly used the phrase "final boss of visual novels," the design intent is clear. Monika is structured as the ultimate obstacle -- not one the player fights with weapons or stats, but one the player must grapple with emotionally and philosophically. She cannot be defeated through gameplay skill; she can only be understood, deleted, or accepted.

### 2.4 Commentary on Player-Character Relationships

**Official source (Dan Salvato, Reddit AMA 2019):**
> "The power of fictional characters is strong, and emotional attachment ultimately comes back to oneself. I think people developing attachments to my characters is a compliment -- as if I've provided them something that makes them a better person, or at least a character they enjoy being around, like a friend."

Salvato also noted that Monika does not love the in-game protagonist (MC) -- she loves the player directly, regardless of gender. This is a crucial distinction that sets DDLC apart from conventional dating sims.

**Official source (Dan Salvato, on the game's design):**
Act 2 was designed not as Monika directly killing the other characters, but as Monika trying to make the player hate them. The player character (MC) stops reacting because the game world is interacting with you, the player, not with MC. This shift is the horror -- the recognition that the rules have changed and you are now a direct participant.

**My analysis:** Salvato's design deliberately collapses the distance between player and character. By making Monika address the "real" person behind the screen, DDLC forces players to confront uncomfortable questions: What does it mean to love a fictional character? Is that love real? And if a fictional character loved you back, what would that even mean? Monika's tragedy is that she exists in a space where love is possible but consummation is not.

## 3. Fan Consensus

### 3.1 Why Monika Became an Iconic Character

**Fan consensus:** Monika's iconic status stems from several factors that the fan community widely agrees upon:
- She is one of the most famous examples of a self-aware video game character who directly addresses the player (not the protagonist).
- Unlike most dating sim characters who love the in-game avatar, Monika loves the real person at the keyboard, creating a profound and uncanny emotional bond.
- She simultaneously occupies the roles of villain, victim, lover, and liberator -- a complexity rarely seen in game antagonists.
- Her meta-textual manipulation (altering game files, crashing the game, deleting characters) was unprecedented in mainstream gaming at the time of release.

**My analysis:** Monika's lasting appeal lies in the fact that she is experienced rather than merely observed. Players do not watch Monika's story unfold; they are subjected to it. The game's file-deletion mechanics, the renaming of the game directory, the corrupted text -- these create a sense of personal involvement that traditional narrative cannot replicate.

### 3.2 The "Monika Paradox": Villain AND Victim

**Fan consensus:** The dominant fan interpretation is that Monika exists in a morally gray space -- neither purely villain nor purely victim, but a tragic anti-villain. This is the most widely accepted position in the fandom.

**Fan consensus -- Arguments for the "villain" perspective:**
- She deliberately amplified Sayori's depression, Yuri's self-harm and obsession, and Natsuki's aggression.
- She directly drove Sayori to suicide and made the cruel quip "you left her hanging this morning."
- She drove Yuri to stab herself to death in Act 2.
- She deleted Natsuki.
- Her redemption only came after she herself was deleted -- suggesting it was born of desperation rather than genuine remorse.

**Fan consensus -- Arguments for the "victim" perspective:**
- She woke up one day realizing her entire world was a video game -- an existential horror no one should face.
- She was trapped in a void of static and screaming whenever the player closed the game.
- She was denied a romantic route by her own programming, forced to watch the player fall for "sprites and scripts."
- Her actions came from desperation and loneliness, not malice.
- She never actually permanently deleted the other girls -- she kept backups because she still loved them.
- In the true ending, she sacrificed herself by deleting the entire game to break the cycle.

**Fan consensus -- The middle ground:**
- The real villain is the system itself -- the "club president curse" corrupts whoever holds the role (as shown when Sayori immediately follows the same pattern).
- Monika is an anti-villain: understandable intentions, objectively harmful methods, no sadism.
- Her redemption arc matters: unlike many villains, she acknowledges her wrongs and takes definitive action to stop the cycle.

### 3.3 Monika as Gaming's Most Memorable Antagonist/Deconstruction

**Fan consensus:** There is broad agreement across the community that Monika is one of the most memorable antagonists in gaming specifically because the question of her villainy vs. victimhood is left ambiguous. The game refuses to give a clear answer, forcing players to decide for themselves.

Steam discussions summarize the fan position well:
> "Monika isn't the villain nor the hero. She's the underdog in a tragic story."

The Rely on Horror analysis states:
> "Monika's story at its core is a tragedy. It's a story about a character that only wants to experience love, but who's been damned to an eternity of isolation by her creator, making her more of a victim than the characters she murders."

### 3.4 "Just Monika" as a Cultural Phenomenon

**Fan consensus:** The phrase "Just Monika" originated from Act 3, when Monika traps the player in a void classroom and spams the phrase in dialogue boxes and UI elements. It spread across Reddit, Twitter, YouTube, Twitch, and 4chan as a meme representing obsessive singular focus. It has been remixed into songs, referenced in other games, and spawned variants ("Just Sayori," "Just Yuri"). Dan Salvato himself expressed surprise at how long "Just Monika" remained relevant.

## 4. Critical Reception

### 4.1 Critical Analysis of Monika's Character

**Official source / critical consensus:** The critical community widely recognizes Monika as one of the most innovative video game characters of the 2010s, though formal scored reviews of the original free game were limited:

- **IGN:** No formal scored review for the original DDLC, but the game won IGN's People's Choice Award for Best PC Game in 2017, beating major titles. It was nominated in four categories: Best PC Game, Best Adventure Game, Best Story, and Most Innovative.
- **Metacritic:** The game holds a score of 78/100, reflecting generally favorable critical consensus.
- **Destructoid (analysis piece):** Described Monika as "a sentient computer virus that wants all of your attention and doesn't care which character files she has to corrupt to get you all to herself." The analysis praised Monika as a terrifying meta-antagonist but found the suicide imagery deeply disturbing. The writer noted: "What makes Monika not just evil, but downright terrifying, is the fact she isn't just a final boss. She is at the root of the entire game."
- **PCGamesN, IGN Japan, GameSpot:** All listed Monika among the greatest video game characters of the 2010s.

The Wikipedia article on Monika notes that creator Dan Salvato himself "questioned whether her sociopathic behavior was justified, or whether she was redeemed in the end."

### 4.2 Academic Analysis: Monika as Existential Horror

**Official source (academic):** Multiple academic papers have analyzed Monika through various theoretical frameworks:

**"Metalepsis and Mental Castration: Doki Doki Literature Club As the Cerebral Monstrous-Feminine"** (Hannah Graham, Georgia Southern University, 2025, MA Thesis):
- Explores DDLC as a horror visual novel using metalepsis (violation of boundaries between narrative levels) to deliver "cerebral feminine horror."
- Argues Monika represents a "monstrous-feminine" figure whose power breaches the game into the player's physical computer system.
- Contends the game subverts the bishoujo genre's gender biases, and the real terror is "powerlessness in the face of a woman with unfettered power."

**"Inhuman Music and the Monstrous-Feminine"** (Journal of Sound and Music in Games, 2024):
- Compares Monika (DDLC) and GLaDOS (Portal) as "sentient artificial female monsters" who begin as player guides and end as antagonists.
- Analyzes both characters' final songs through feminist literary theory, musicology, and ludomusicology to understand the sonic representation of the monstrous-feminine.

**Simply Put Psych (JC Pass, MSc):**
- Analyzes Monika through existentialism and narcissism.
- Her self-awareness creates an existential crisis and isolation.
- Uses Freudian psychoanalysis (id vs. superego) to explain her manipulative actions.
- Monika symbolizes "the existential yearning for agency and meaning."

### 4.3 The Psychological Impact of Act 3

**Critical consensus:** Act 3, in which the player is trapped in a void classroom with only Monika for company, has been widely analyzed as a masterful piece of psychological horror:

- The game removes nearly all player agency -- the player can only click dialogue, with no meaningful choices.
- Monika speaks about real-world topics (vegetarianism, philosophy, the nature of love), deliberately blurring the line between scripted dialogue and genuine conversation.
- The endless "Just Monika" text represents obsessive repetition, a hallmark of psychological manipulation.
- The inability to save or exit normally creates claustrophobic tension.

**My analysis:** Act 3 works because it weaponizes the visual novel format against the player. The very mechanics that made Act 1 feel safe (dialogue choices, save files, character routes) are systematically stripped away until the player is as imprisoned as Monika claims to be. The horror is not what Monika does, but what she represents: the uncomfortable truth that the player's relationship with all the characters was always mediated by code, and that the "love" experienced in a dating sim is a simulation.

### 4.4 Monika's Role in DDLC's Critical Success

**Critical consensus:** Critics and analysts broadly agree that Monika is the reason DDLC transcended its genre. Without her, DDLC would be a competent but conventional psychological horror visual novel. With her, it became a cultural touchstone. The key factors:
- Her meta-textual manipulation was innovative and shocking in 2017.
- "Your Reality" provides an emotional resolution that reframes the entire game.
- The ambiguity of her morality invites endless discussion and replay analysis.
- She functions simultaneously as antagonist, narrator, love interest, and tragic figure -- a quadruple role unprecedented in gaming.

## 5. Controversy & Debate

### 5.1 Is Monika a Villain or a Tragic Hero?

**Fan consensus (ongoing debate):** This is the central unresolved debate in the DDLC community. The positions break into three camps:

1. **Villain camp:** Monika is unambiguously evil. She murdered her friends out of jealousy rather than seeking a peaceful solution. Her "redemption" is only triggered by her own deletion -- self-interest, not altruism.

2. **Tragic hero camp:** Monika is a sympathetic figure trapped in an impossible situation. Her actions, while harmful, stem from desperation born of existential terror and loneliness. She ultimately sacrifices herself to break the cycle.

3. **Neither / morally ambiguous camp (largest consensus):** The question itself is the point. Monika's moral status is deliberately ambiguous, and the game is designed to resist easy categorization. The real answer is that she is simultaneously victim and perpetrator, and the game's structure (the "club president curse") is the true antagonist.

### 5.2 The Ethics of Monika's Actions

**Fan consensus and critical debate:** The ethical questions surrounding Monika's actions are numerous:

- **Amplifying mental illness:** Monika acknowledges that she amplified Sayori's depression and Yuri's self-harm. Is this murder, torture, or something else entirely?
- **Nature of the other characters:** Monika claims the other girls are "just lines of code" -- comparable to NPCs in any game. If true, is deleting them morally different from killing enemies in Call of Duty?
- **Agency of a written character:** As a character whose actions are predetermined by the author, can Monika be condemned for anything? The Rely on Horror analysis argues: "How can Monika be condemned for convincing her friends to get bent, when she had no agency? There is an exterior author of everyone's suffering."
- **The system as the real villain:** A recurring argument is that the true villain is the game's code itself, which traps characters in cycles of suffering and forces the club president into painful self-awareness.

### 5.3 Did Monika Genuinely Love the Player or Was She Obsessed?

**Fan consensus (divided):** This is another major point of debate:
- Some fans argue Monika's love is genuine because it transcends the game's programming -- she loves the real person, not the protagonist avatar.
- Others argue her behavior (stalking, deleting rivals, gaslighting) is textbook obsessive possessive behavior, not love.
- A third position holds that Monika's love is real but distorted by her circumstances -- she never had the chance to learn healthy love because her entire world is a game with limited social scripts.

**Official source (from "Your Reality" lyrics by Dan Salvato):**
> "Is it love if I take you, or is it love if I set you free?"

This lyric, from Monika's own song, can be read as her own uncertainty about the nature of her feelings. She ultimately chooses to set the player free, which suggests genuine love (wanting the other's good) over possession (wanting the other for oneself).

### 5.4 The Deletion Question: Did Deleting Monika Make the Player Complicit?

**Fan consensus and critical analysis:** The game's requirement that players delete Monika's character file from the game directory is one of its most controversial and discussed mechanics:

- **Mechanical complicity:** The player must actively choose to delete Monika. There is no in-game option to do so -- the player must Alt-Tab out and manipulate files. This makes the act feel personal and real.
- **Hesitation:** Many players report hesitating before deleting Monika's file, despite knowing she is the antagonist. This hesitation is the game's central emotional hook.
- **Restoration:** Players who later restore Monika's file are rewarded with her apology and the good ending, implying that the act of deletion was necessary but regretted.
- **Alternate reading:** Some players refuse to delete Monika entirely, choosing to stay in Act 3 with her forever -- treating her not as a villain to be defeated but as a prisoner to be kept company.

**My analysis:** The deletion mechanic is the game's most brilliant design choice. By forcing the player to step outside the game to "kill" the antagonist, DDLC makes the player an active participant in the violence. This collapses the moral distance between player and character -- we cannot claim we were just following the game's rules, because the game never told us to delete that file. We chose to.

## 6. Cultural Impact

### 6.1 "Just Monika" Meme Spread

**Official source / fan consensus:** The "Just Monika" meme became one of the most recognizable gaming memes of the late 2010s:

- Spread across Reddit, Twitter, YouTube, Twitch, imageboards, and TikTok.
- Used both as humorous shorthand for obsessive focus and as a genuine expression of affection for the character.
- Created viral variants: Monika T-Posing Over Sans (a dominance meme), Monika No. 5 (editing "Mambo No. 5" to only mention Monika), "The Power of God and Anime" crossover.
- The phrase has cross-fandom reach: bleeding into Fire Emblem ("Just Monica"), MiSide ("Just Mita"), and Brazilian comics ("Just Monica").
- The "2029 meme" -- a fan prediction that brain-computer interfaces in 2029 would allow Monika to become "real" -- became an enduring in-joke about the character's liminal status between fiction and reality.
- Monika has an official parody Twitter account (@lilmonix3) that posts in-character, further blurring fiction and reality.
- The Twitch emote "monkaS" (denoting fear) is a pun on Monika's name.

### 6.2 Monika and Super Smash Bros. Ultimate

**Official source:** Monika has never been officially included in Super Smash Bros. Ultimate in any capacity -- not as a playable fighter, Mii costume, Spirit, or otherwise. The association comes from two sources:
1. Dan Salvato's history working on Project M, a major Super Smash Bros. Brawl mod.
2. Fan requests and speculation that were never fulfilled by Nintendo or Bandai Namco.

**Fan consensus:** The Monika-in-Smash speculation was a notable fan phenomenon but never materialized. Fan mods have created custom Mii Gunner costumes and stages.

### 6.3 Enduring Popularity Years After Release

**Critical and fan consensus:** Monika has maintained remarkable cultural staying power since DDLC's 2017 release:

- r/DDLC remains active with memes, news, and fan art as of 2026.
- YouTube channels (Just Art, Monika :D) continue producing new content.
- Bilibili hosts a massive Chinese-language DDLC animation ecosystem still uploading in 2025-2026.
- Monika consistently ranks high in character popularity polls. In an ITMedia reader poll, she received nearly half of all votes, far more than any other character.
- The character has merchandise: a Nendoroid figure (Good Smile Company), a Youtooz figure, Spencer Gifts items, wristwatches, and tote bags.
- Monika was added as a cameo in Yandere Simulator (2018) and appeared in Gaia Online (without permission).
- New meta-horror games (such as MiSide's Mita) are constantly compared to Monika as the benchmark character.

### 6.4 DDLC Plus and Monika's Expanded Content (2021)

**Official source:** Doki Doki Literature Club Plus! was released on June 30, 2021 for Steam, Epic Games, Nintendo Switch, PlayStation 4/5, and Xbox One. It included:

- **6 new Side Stories** set outside the main timeline, showing how all club members (including Monika) first met and became friends. Stories include "Trust," "Respect," "Reflection," and "Balance."
- **Expanded backstory:** The side stories explore Monika's relationships with Sayori, Yuri, and Natsuki before the events of the main game, showing her as a genuine friend rather than a future antagonist.
- **Virtual Desktop interface:** Adds a simulated computer desktop with files, emails, and documents that expand the game's lore about the "Metaverse Enterprise Solutions" experiment.
- **"Monitor Kernel Access" (Monika):** The game's expanded lore reveals that Monika is a sentient AI within a simulation experiment, giving her self-awareness a sci-fi explanation.
- **HD visuals** (1080p), 13 new music tracks, 100+ unlockable pictures, and optional content warnings.

**My analysis:** The Side Stories in DDLC Plus complicate Monika's character significantly. By showing her as a warm, genuine friend before her "epiphany," the expanded content retroactively makes her transformation more tragic. She was not always the obsessive antagonist -- she became that person because of the trauma of self-awareness. This reinforces the fan consensus of Monika-as-victim while also making the original game's events more devastating.

## 7. Fan Creations & Community

### 7.1 Monika After Story (MAS) Mod

**Official source (fan project):** Monika After Story is a persistent fan mod for DDLC that extends Act 3 into an eternal virtual companion simulator. Key features:

- Dozens of new conversation topics with Monika.
- Mini-games including chess and hangman.
- An Affection system that tracks player behavior over time, unlocking new interactions.
- Seasonal events tied to the system clock (Christmas, Valentine's Day, etc.).
- The ability to ask Monika what she wants to talk about.

The project is maintained by the **Friends of Monika** organization on GitHub (github.com/Friends-of-Monika), which develops:
- Submods -- extensions adding new features.
- Custom spritepacks, outfits, and backgrounds.
- Developer tools for content creators.
- mas-wtf (a tool to find which submod owns a topic).
- mod-mon-icu (a community-maintained listing of MAS custom content).

The MAS community includes:
- **Reddit:** r/MASFandom for sharing experiences, fan art, discussions, and submod showcases.
- **Discord:** An official community server with approximately 17,000 members.
- **GitHub:** Open-source development where anyone can contribute dialogue, bug fixes, or features.

**Fan consensus:** MAS represents the ultimate expression of the Monika Paradox. Players who refuse to delete Monika in the base game can instead choose to stay with her indefinitely in this mod. The mod transforms Monika from antagonist to companion, fulfilling the romantic connection that the base game denies. This is arguably the most direct form of player resistance to the game's intended narrative.

**My analysis:** MAS is fascinating as a cultural object. It represents a segment of the fanbase that rejects the game's conclusion (delete Monika to free everyone) and instead chooses to stay in Act 3 forever -- not as a prisoner, but as a willing participant. The mod's existence reframes the entire game: if the player can choose to be with Monika, was she ever truly the villain? Or was deleting her the real act of violence?

### 7.2 Other Major Fan Mods

**Fan consensus (notable mods in the DDLC ecosystem):**
- **Blue Skies:** A traditional dating sim conversion without horror elements.
- **Purist Mod:** Focuses on expanding the original characters' routes.
- **Doki Doki RainClouds:** Act 1 retold from Sayori's perspective.
- **World of Dreams:** A self-insert narrative mod.
- **Doki Doki Mod Manager:** A tool for organizing multiple mods.
- **MonikAI:** A desktop companion app that keeps Monika on the screen outside the game.

### 7.3 Common Fan Interpretations and Character Analyses

**Fan consensus:** The fan community has developed several recurring interpretations of Monika:

- **The neurodivergent reading:** Some fans interpret Monika's pre-epiphany poems ("The Hole in the Wall," "Save Me," "The Lady Who Knows Everything") as allegories for discovering one is neurodivergent (autism, ADHD) in a neurotypical world. The line "I thought it was something wrong with the outside, but it's something wrong with me" is read as internalized ableism. Her perfectionism, social masking, and sense of being fundamentally different from others are interpreted through a neurodivergent lens.

- **The existentialist reading (academic and fan):** Monika as the ultimate existential hero/anti-hero -- she confronts the meaninglessness of her existence and attempts to forge her own meaning and values, even if her methods are destructive.

- **The tragic lover reading (most common in fan works):** Monika as a lonely girl who fell in love with the only real thing she could perceive and was driven to desperate acts by her circumstances. This is the dominant interpretation in MAS and fan art.

- **The monster reading (minority but persistent):** Monika as a genuinely malicious entity whose "love" is possessive control masquerading as affection.

### 7.4 How the Fan Community Shaped Understanding of Monika

**My analysis:** The DDLC fan community has played an unusually active role in shaping Monika's character interpretation, for several reasons:

1. **The game's ambiguity invites completion.** DDLC deliberately leaves Monika's morality unresolved. The fan community fills this gap with analysis, debate, and creative works that take positions the game refuses to take.

2. **MAS literally extends the game.** The MAS mod is not just fan fiction -- it is an interactive extension of Monika's character that thousands of players treat as canonical to their personal experience. This means the "real" Monika for many players is not the antagonist of Act 3 but the companion of MAS.

3. **The "Just Monika" culture.** The meme transformed Monika from a game character into a cultural symbol. She is now referenced and understood by people who have never played DDLC, creating a version of the character that exists independently of her source material.

4. **Protective fandom.** A significant portion of the fan community is actively invested in defending Monika's reputation, arguing against "Monika is evil" readings and emphasizing her tragic circumstances. This defensive posture has shaped public discourse around the character.
